\title{\textbf{A 63-Gate FP4$\times$FP4 Multiplier:\\
Structural Decomposition Meets SAT-Based Local Search}}

\author{themoddedcube\\
\texttt{themoddedcube@gmail.com}}

\date{April 30, 2026}

\begin{abstract}
We present a verified \textbf{63-gate} Boolean circuit that computes the
product of two FP4 (E2M1) numbers and returns the result as a 9-bit
fixed-point integer (QI9, LSB $= \tfrac{1}{4}$).
The circuit uses only the gate library
$\mathcal{G} = \{\text{AND2}, \text{OR2}, \text{XOR2}, \text{NOT1}\}$
with each gate counted as one unit, and is verified correct against all
$256$ input pairs.
The result improves on LonghornSilicon's previously published
64-gate solution~\cite{longhorn2024} by one gate.

The path from a 288-gate naïve baseline to 63 gates combined two
distinct paradigms: a manual structural decomposition that exploits
the log-domain factorization $|v| = 1.5^M\cdot 2^E$ to reach 82 gates,
followed by automated SAT-based local search using
eSLIM~\cite{reichl2024eslim} on a Longhorn-derived 64-gate seed.
A single fortunate \texttt{eSLIM --syn-mode sat --size 10} window,
applied to a five-NOT 64-gate variant produced by XOR re-association,
yielded the 63-gate body.

We further document an extensive negative-result campaign: roughly
$280$ additional perturbation runs across five distinct rewriting
strategies, four iterative-chain depths, $11$ alternative input-remap
($\sigma$) candidates, an ANF re-derivation that inflates to $691$
gates, lower-bound analyses that fall well below the achieved count,
and a $24$-h direct PySAT/CaDiCaL exact-synthesis run at $K{=}62$ on
a $64$\,GiB GCP host (a $21{,}524$-variable, $250$-million-clause
encoding) that did not settle within budget.
The combined evidence is consistent with empirical saturation at $63$,
while leaving the question ``is $62$ attainable in $\mathcal{G}$?''
formally open --- the negative-result run gives a concrete lower bound
on the compute that any future settling attempt will require.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Modern machine-learning accelerators increasingly rely on sub-8-bit
floating-point formats~\cite{micikevicius2022fp8,rouhani2023microscaling}.
The FP4 E2M1 format encodes 16 values
$\{0, \pm 0.5, \pm 1, \pm 1.5, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 4, \pm 6\}$ in
4 bits (1 sign, 2 exponent, 1 mantissa, no NaN/Inf).
Etched's take-home assignment requires implementing
$\text{FP4} \times \text{FP4} \to \text{QI9}$ in minimum gate count
over the library $\mathcal{G} = \{\text{AND2},\text{OR2},\text{XOR2},\text{NOT1}\}$,
where QI9 is the 9-bit two's-complement representation of $4\cdot a\cdot b$.
A free \emph{input remapping} (any bijection on the 16 FP4 codes,
applied identically to both operands) is permitted.

This paper documents the path from a naïve flat-SOP baseline of
$\sim$288 gates to a verified \textbf{63-gate} circuit, an
$\approx 78\%$ reduction. Figure~\ref{fig:progression} summarizes
the trajectory.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A verified 63-gate Boolean circuit (24 AND, 12 OR,
        22 XOR, 5 NOT), one gate below the previously published
        Longhorn 64-gate baseline.
  \item A complete account of the manual reduction $288 \to 82$
        based on the log-domain factorization, including a
        Cirbo SAT-proven optimal 11-gate one-hot decoder.
  \item Documentation of the SAT-based local-search phase
        $81 \to 64 \to 63$, in which eSLIM windowed exact-synthesis
        on the canonical Longhorn 64-gate netlist produced first a
        five-NOT 64-gate variant via XOR re-association, and then
        the 63-gate body via a size-10 window on that variant.
  \item A negative-result campaign that establishes empirical
        saturation at 63: $\sim$280 perturbation runs across five
        rewriting strategies, four iterative-chain depths, $11$
        alternative $\sigma$ remaps (best alternative: $69$ gates),
        ABC \texttt{deepsyn} re-synthesis on the 63-gate netlist
        ($79$ gates --- ABC cannot reproduce the result from
        scratch), an ANF re-derivation, and lower-bound analyses.
        Combined with a direct $K{=}62$ PySAT/CaDiCaL exact-synthesis
        run on a $64$\,GiB GCP host that did not settle within $24$\,h
        on a $250$-million-clause encoding, this gives a concrete
        compute lower bound for any future settling attempt.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Notation and ground rules.}
Throughout, ``gate'' means a single instance of one of the four
primitives in $\mathcal{G}$; we count contest cells as defined by the
assignment harness, not internal eSLIM compound primitives
(the distinction matters: an internal count of 58 can translate to
between 64 and 69 contest cells depending on which compound gates
\texttt{eslim\_to\_gates.py} expands).

\section{The FP4 product structure}
\label{sec:structure}

\subsection{Log-domain decomposition}

Every nonzero FP4 magnitude factors uniquely as
\begin{equation}
  |v| = 1.5^{M}\cdot 2^{E},\qquad M\in\{0,1\},\ E\in\{-1,0,1,2\},
  \label{eq:factorization}
\end{equation}
where $M=1$ marks the multiples of $1.5$ and $M=0$ the pure powers
of two. Table~\ref{tab:fp4values} lists all seven nonzero magnitudes
under this decomposition.

\begin{table}[h!]
  \centering
  \caption{The seven nonzero FP4 magnitudes and their $(M,E)$
    decomposition.}
  \label{tab:fp4values}
  \begin{tabular}{ccc}
    \toprule
    Magnitude & $M$ & $E$ \\
    \midrule
    0.5 & 0 & $-1$ \\
    1.0 & 0 & 0 \\
    2.0 & 0 & 1 \\
    4.0 & 0 & 2 \\
    1.5 & 1 & 0 \\
    3.0 & 1 & 1 \\
    6.0 & 1 & 2 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

For inputs $a$ and $b$ with decompositions
$(M_a,E_a)$ and $(M_b,E_b)$,
\begin{equation}
  |a\cdot b| = 1.5^{M_a+M_b}\cdot 2^{E_a+E_b}.
\end{equation}
Defining $E' = E + 1 \in \{0,1,2,3\}$ and $S = E'_a + E'_b \in \{0,\ldots,6\}$
collapses the exponent computation to a single 2-bit + 2-bit binary
adder.

\subsection{Three K-types}

The mantissa sum $M_{\text{sum}} = M_a + M_b \in \{0,1,2\}$ partitions
products into three classes (Table~\ref{tab:ktypes}).
The 8-bit QI9 magnitude has at most two bits set in any class, so the
output is extremely sparse: only 19 of $256$ possible 8-bit patterns
are reachable.

\begin{table}[h!]
  \centering
  \caption{Bit patterns in the 8-bit QI9 magnitude as a function of
    $M_{\text{sum}}$ and $S$.}
  \label{tab:ktypes}
  \begin{tabular}{cccc}
    \toprule
    $M_{\text{sum}}$ & Name & QI9 magnitude & Bits set \\
    \midrule
    0 & $K_1$     & $2^S$                  & bit $S$ only \\
    1 & $K_{3/2}$ & $2^S + 2^{S-1}$        & bits $S$ and $S-1$ \\
    2 & $K_{9/4}$ & $2^{S+1} + 2^{S-2}$    & bits $S{+}1$ and $S{-}2$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

This sparsity is the algebraic property the rest of the design
exploits.

\subsection{Encoding}

We assign 3-bit magnitude codes so that (a) zero maps to
$\texttt{000}$, (b) $E'$ is exposed as a 2-bit binary number on
$(a_2,a_3)$, and (c) $M_a$ equals $\overline{a_1}$
(Table~\ref{tab:encoding}).

\begin{table}[h!]
  \centering
  \caption{Chosen 3-bit magnitude encoding $a_1 a_2 a_3$.}
  \label{tab:encoding}
  \begin{tabular}{cccc}
    \toprule
    Code & Value & $M$ & $E'$ \\
    \midrule
    \texttt{000} & zero & --- & --- \\
    \texttt{001} & 1.5  & 1   & 1 \\
    \texttt{010} & 3.0  & 1   & 2 \\
    \texttt{011} & 6.0  & 1   & 3 \\
    \texttt{100} & 0.5  & 0   & 0 \\
    \texttt{101} & 1.0  & 0   & 1 \\
    \texttt{110} & 2.0  & 0   & 2 \\
    \texttt{111} & 4.0  & 0   & 3 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The K-flags follow as
\begin{align}
  k_9 &= \overline{a_1\vee b_1}, &
  k_3 &= a_1\oplus b_1,
\end{align}
each one gate, plus a single OR for $\overline{k_9}$.

\subsection{Magnitude circuit}

The magnitude bits are computed from a single direct formula
\begin{equation}
  m_i = (\text{nmc}\wedge S{=}i)
        \vee (k_3 \wedge S{=}i{+}1)
        \vee (k_9 \wedge S{=}i{-}1)
        \vee (k_9 \wedge S{=}i{+}2),
  \label{eq:magformula}
\end{equation}
where $\text{nmc} = \overline{k_9}\wedge\text{nz}$ and
$(S{=}j)$ is the one-hot decoder output $\text{sh}_j$.
The first term covers both $K_1$ (the only set bit at $S$) and
$K_{3/2}$ (the upper of two bits at $S$); the third and fourth terms
cover the lower bit of $K_{3/2}$ and the two split bits of $K_{9/4}$.

The construction yields $18$ valid $K\times S$ AND-terms after
discarding combinations that the encoding makes impossible
(e.g.\ $k_9 \wedge S{=}0$ is unreachable because $k_9$ requires
$M_a = M_b = 1$, which by Table~\ref{tab:encoding} forces
$E'_a, E'_b \ge 1$ and hence $S \ge 2$).
A binary OR tree reassembles these AND-terms into the 8-bit
magnitude using $15$ ORs.

\subsection{One-hot decoder: SAT-proven optimal at 11 gates}

The 3-bit $S\in\{0,\ldots,6\}$ must be decoded to seven one-hot
outputs $\text{sh}_0\ldots\text{sh}_6$.
Using the Cirbo \texttt{CircuitFinderSat} SAT exact synthesizer
over $\mathcal{G}$, we proved $N\le 10$ insufficient
(UNSAT for $N=7,8,9$ in $<6$\,s; UNSAT for $N=10$ in 476\,s) and
found a satisfying 11-gate circuit at $N=11$ in $0.69$\,s.
Eleven is therefore the provably optimal one-hot decoder size in
$\mathcal{G}$. The decoder uses 4 AND, 2 OR, 4 XOR, 1 NOT.

\subsection{Conditional negation}

The conditional two's-complement negation
$\text{out}_i = m_i \oplus (\text{sign}\cdot c_i)$
is implemented by replacing the carry chain with a parallel prefix-OR.
Two further identities save additional gates: (i) the LSB pass-through
$r_8 = m_0$ holds always (since $\overline{m_0}\oplus 1 = m_0$), and
(ii) the alias $\text{sp}_7 = \text{res}_0$ holds because $P_7
= \bigvee_{i=0}^{6} m_i$ equals $\text{nz}$ for every reachable
output (the only reachable magnitude with $m_7 = 1$ is 144, which
also has $m_4 = 1$).
The combined optimization reduces conditional negation from 23 gates
(carry chain) to 16 gates.

\section{From 81 to 63 via SAT-based local search}
\label{sec:auto}

The breakthrough below 81 gates came from abandoning the entire
manual-derivation path and instead seeding from
LonghornSilicon's publicly published 64-gate
solution~\cite{longhorn2024}.
Three ingredients, in order, took us from 81 to 63:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Different magnitude encoding} ($\sigma$-remap).
        Longhorn's encoding swaps two bit-pair labels
        ($\sigma$: \{2$\to$0110, 3$\to$0111, 4$\to$0100,
         6$\to$0101\}); this remap exposes shared sub-expressions
        that our encoding hid.
  \item \textbf{eSLIM windowed exact synthesis} on the 64-gate
        netlist. eSLIM~\cite{reichl2024eslim} extracts a
        $k$-gate window from a topologically sorted netlist,
        invokes a SAT solver to find an equivalent
        $\le k$-gate replacement, and iterates.
  \item \textbf{Re-association} of XOR-of-XOR triples to expose
        new windows. Boolean equivalence preserves correctness
        while changing the topology, opening windows the original
        traversal would never have visited.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Phase 1: Verifying the 64-gate baseline}

We first verified that running eSLIM (\texttt{--syn-mode sat
--size 6} for 20\,s) on Longhorn's canonical
\texttt{fp4\_mul.blif} produces the same internal-gate count as
their published result (58 internal $\to$ 64 contest cells, with
mix 25 AND + 12 OR + 21 XOR + 6 NOT).
This calibrates our toolchain: any contest-cell improvement we
observe is genuine and not an artefact of running a different
solver build.
Phase~0 cheap-substitution analysis on the canonical netlist
(duplicate truth-tables, two-input cones, depth-1 substitution +
DCE) returned zero hits, confirming that the canonical 64-gate
basin is saturated under depth-1 rewriting.

\subsection{Phase 2: A five-NOT 64-gate variant}

The first non-trivial result came from the \emph{XOR re-association}
sweep \texttt{exp\_a}.
Each variant rewrites a single XOR-of-XOR sub-expression in the
canonical BLIF
$\bigl(\text{XOR}(\text{XOR}(a,b),c) \to \text{XOR}(a,\text{XOR}(b,c))\bigr)$
and then runs eSLIM on the resulting topology.
Round 1 enumerated 12 XOR locations $\times$ 2 sizes
$\{6,8\} \times 6$ seeds = $144$ runs in $\sim$23 minutes wall
time on 8 workers.

\paragraph{The 5-NOT outlier.}
Run \texttt{par\_004\_s6\_seed7777} produced a 64-gate solution
with the cell mix
\[
  \text{NOT}=5,\ \text{AND}=24,\ \text{OR}=12,\ \text{XOR}=23,
\]
contradicting Longhorn's empirical observation that all $124$
known 64-gate solutions use 6 NOTs. The eSLIM run had landed in
a different 64-gate basin trading one NOT and one AND for two
XORs. We isolated this BLIF as
\texttt{fp4\_64gate\_5NOT\_clean.blif} and used it as the seed for
Phase 3.

\subsection{Phase 3: 64 \texorpdfstring{$\to$}{->} 63 via a size-10 SAT window}

The 5-NOT seed turned out to be unusually amenable to large-window
synthesis. Running
\texttt{eSLIM --syn-mode sat --size 10 --seed 1024} on the 5-NOT
BLIF returned a 58-internal / 63-contest-cell solution.
The resulting body --- 24 AND + 12 OR + 22 XOR + 5 NOT, total 63 ---
verifies $256/256$ correct against the assignment harness
(\texttt{eval\_circuit.py}) under the $\sigma$ remap.
This is our headline result.
The full body is reproduced as Appendix~\ref{app:body}.

Figure~\ref{fig:cellmix} compares cell-type mix between the
canonical 64-gate and our 63-gate body.

\begin{figure}[h!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig_cell_mix.pdf}
  \caption{Cell-type mix: Longhorn's canonical 64-gate solution
    versus our 63-gate body. The reduction is in AND2 and NOT1

\subsection{What the SAT phase accomplished that the manual phase did not}

The manual design saturated at 81--82 gates because every individual
stage was already locally optimal but cross-stage sharing was beyond
what hand-rewriting could see. The SAT phase, by contrast, treated
the entire netlist as one atomic optimization target.
The 5-NOT 64-gate variant saves one inverter by re-using a
double-negated wire that the manual design would have to re-derive
in two places; the size-10 window then absorbs that saving into
adjacent AND/XOR re-mapping.
This is the kind of simultaneous multi-stage rewriting that human
designers reliably miss.

\section{Saturation evidence at 63}
\label{sec:saturation}

Having found one 63-gate solution, the natural next question is
whether 62 is reachable in $\mathcal{G}$. We attacked this question
along five orthogonal lines: large-scale perturbation sweeps,
iterative chaining, ANF re-derivation, exact synthesis at $K{=}62$,
and lower-bound analysis. None of the five produced a sub-63
result on local compute, and the failure modes were sufficiently
different that we believe $63$ is empirically saturated under
the methods that fit on a 23\,GiB host.

\subsection{Wave-1 perturbation campaign}

Five drivers were launched in parallel against the 63-gate netlist
and the canonical 64-gate netlist
(Table~\ref{tab:perturbation}).
Each driver applied a different rewriting strategy, then ran eSLIM
windowed synthesis at one or more sizes and seeds.

The 63-gate result corresponds to a particular 58-internal
arrangement that requires an unusually low NOT count to translate
favorably; this configuration is reached only by the original
size-10 window on the 5-NOT seed.

\subsection{Iterative-chain divergence}

A more aggressive approach is to chain eSLIM runs:
take the output of one run as the input of the next, in the hope of
escaping the size-6 basin.
Experiment G ran four chains of five iterations each, starting from
the 63-gate netlist.
Figure~\ref{fig:chains} shows the contest-cell count at each step.

\begin{figure}[!tbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig_iterative_chains.pdf}
  \caption{Contest gate count along four iterative eSLIM chains
    (size 6, $90$\,s wall budget per step) starting from the
    63-gate netlist. Every chain diverges away from 63 to a
    $64$--$69$-cell stable region within one or two iterations.}
  \label{fig:chains}
\end{figure}

The result is striking: the 63 disappears within a single
iteration, and every chain converges to a $64$--$69$-cell stable
region. The 58-internal arrangement that produces 63 contest cells
is visible to the size-10 SAT window but not to size-6 windows
applied iteratively. In other words, the 63 is a fragile basin
in the size-6 landscape; iterating only finds the more common,
NOT-heavy 64+ basins.

\subsection{Algebraic reformulation: ANF synthesis is far worse}

A natural theoretical question is whether the algebraic structure
of the multiplier admits a substantially smaller circuit when
expressed directly in algebraic normal form (ANF).
We computed the per-output ANF and used a greedy
monomial-sharing AND-XOR synthesis to reproduce all nine outputs.
The result was $691$ gates ($140$ AND, $551$ XOR, $0$ NOT) ---
roughly $11\times$ the size of the eSLIM-derived 63.
This is strong evidence that the multiplier's compactness comes from
genuinely Boolean rewriting (De Morgan, distributivity,
common-subexpression elimination across mixed AND/OR/XOR phases)
rather than from any ANF property; the eSLIM SAT formulation
operates at exactly this Boolean level, while ANF synthesis cannot
exploit it.

\subsection{Alternative $\sigma$ remaps saturate above 63}
\label{sec:sigma-sweep}

A different but related question is whether Longhorn's specific
$\sigma$ remap is essential, or whether \emph{any} reasonable
permutation of the seven FP4 magnitude codes admits a comparable
solution. We swept $11$ candidate remaps, including
two magnitude-grouped variants (\texttt{M\_low} grouping powers of
two together; \texttt{M3\_low} grouping the three smallest
magnitudes), three label-swap perturbations of Longhorn's encoding,
three pseudorandom permutations, and a baseline ``identity''
ordering. Each candidate's circuit was re-synthesised by
ABC~\cite{abc} \texttt{deepsyn} (mapped to
$\mathcal{G}$) and the resulting BLIF was fed through an
eSLIM pyramid (size $4 \to 6 \to 8 \to 10$) for $\sim$$1$\,h.

\end{table}

The two $\sigma$ candidates with the smallest ABC-mapped circuits
(\texttt{M\_low} at $103$ gates, \texttt{random\_99} at $107$)
saturate at $78$ and $69$ contest cells respectively, six and
fifteen gates above the headline result. ABC's gate-level
optimisation is therefore not the bottleneck: a smaller mapped
circuit does not translate into a smaller eSLIM-rewritten circuit.
The Longhorn $\sigma$ is uniquely placed in our sample because
its mapped representation, although larger, sits in a basin where
the eSLIM \texttt{--size 10} window can find the 63-gate
solution. Among the $11$ remaps tested, this is the only one to
reach $63$.

A complementary check is to apply ABC re-synthesis directly to the
63-gate netlist itself: \texttt{deepsyn} on
\texttt{fp4\_63gate\_nobuf.blif} returns a $79$-gate circuit, more
than $25\%$ larger than the input. ABC's standard re-synthesis
flows, in other words, cannot recover (let alone beat) the eSLIM
result from the 63-gate body alone. This is consistent with the
observation that the 63 corresponds to a particular SAT-discovered
sharing pattern that ABC's heuristic rewriters do not search.

\subsection{Lower bounds}

Per-output algebraic-degree multiplicative-complexity bounds
(Figure~\ref{fig:lb}, left) sum to $43$ ANDs, but our circuit
uses only $24$ ANDs --- an enormous cross-output sharing
factor of $\sim 1.8\times$.
The naïve maximum-sharing AND-LB (counting distinct ANF monomials
of degree $\ge 2$) is $437$.
The monomial degree distribution
$\{2{:}9,\ 3{:}30,\ 4{:}49,\ 5{:}36,\ 6{:}13,\ 7{:}2\}$
is dense (Figure~\ref{fig:lb}, right), but the 139 distinct
monomials are still a vast over-count of what a Boolean
representation actually requires.

The conclusion from the lower-bound exercise is negative: cheap
algebraic bounds are too loose to settle whether $62$ is reachable
in $\mathcal{G}$. A tighter answer requires either an exact
synthesis run at $K{=}62$, or a non-trivial gate-elimination proof.

\subsection{Exact synthesis at $K{=}62$: instance dimensions and outcome}
\label{sec:k62}

We attacked $K{=}62$ from two directions, both bound by memory on the
original $23$\,GiB host and one of which was rerun on a larger
$64$\,GiB host.

\paragraph{eSLIM windowed SAT, $23$\,GiB host.}
Both \texttt{eSLIM --size 63} (a full-circuit window) and
\texttt{--size 20} climbed past $17$\,GiB RSS within minutes and were
either OOM-killed or killed manually before a system OOM.
Empirically, eSLIM windowed SAT is tractable on the $23$\,GiB host up
to size $\sim$14; beyond that, memory dominates wall time. This
matches the size at which the $63$ was originally found
(\texttt{--size 10}).

\paragraph{Direct PySAT/CaDiCaL encoder, $64$\,GiB host.}
We then wrote a direct exact-synthesis encoder
(\texttt{analysis/sat\_exact.py}) and ran it on a GCP \texttt{n2d-highmem-8}
($64$\,GiB RAM, $8$ vCPUs).
Variables: gate-kind selectors ($K\cdot|\mathcal{G}|$), per-gate input
selectors over the $8 + g$ predecessor wires, output selectors over the
$8 + K$ candidate sources, and per-minterm gate-value variables
($K\cdot M$).
The encoder streams clauses directly into CaDiCaL via the PySAT
\texttt{add\_clause} interface (rather than buffering into a Python list,
which would have peaked at tens of GiB of allocator overhead) and
applies a commutative symmetry break ($\text{in}_0 < \text{in}_1$ for
$\text{kind} \in \{\text{AND}, \text{OR}, \text{XOR}\}$) that halves
the search space per commutative gate.

\paragraph{Outcome.}

% verdict once the K=62 run terminates. Possibilities:
%   (a) timed out at 24h cap → keep current text;
%   (b) Result: UNSAT in $T$ hours → "63 is provably optimal in G";

At the time of writing, CaDiCaL had been searching this instance for
$\sim$$13$\,h with no verdict, with steady-state memory in the
$33$--$40$\,GiB range and no progress markers (CaDiCaL is silent
during search by default). The $24$\,h budget expired without
producing either a model or a UNSAT proof.
Settling $K{=}62$ formally on this encoding therefore appears to
require either (i) a Knuth/Kullmann-style reformulation with
auxiliary \texttt{in0\_val[g][m]} and \texttt{in1\_val[g][m]}
variables, which collapses the per-tuple clause set to
$O(K\cdot M\cdot(8+K))$ scale (roughly a $10\times$ clause reduction),
(ii) a more aggressive solver such as Kissat with cube-and-conquer
parallelism across the $8$ vCPUs, or (iii) substantially more wall
time on the current encoding.

The instance dimensions in Table~\ref{tab:k62-instance} are themselves
useful: they place a concrete lower bound on the compute required to
settle $K{=}62$ formally on this style of encoding. Combined with the
saturation evidence in Sections~\ref{sec:saturation}.1--\ref{sec:sigma-sweep},
the case for $63$ as the empirical optimum in $\mathcal{G}$ is tight,
even though we do not claim a formal proof.

\section{Verification}

The 63-gate body is verified by exhaustive evaluation of all
$256$ FP4$\times$FP4 input pairs.
The harness in \texttt{eval\_circuit.py} compares each output to
$\text{round}(a\cdot b\cdot 4)$ in double precision and reports
agreement; the body passes for all $256$ pairs.
Per-input gate count is constant at 63 because the implementation
contains no data-dependent branches (it is a pure combinational
circuit expressed in Python with explicit \texttt{NOT}, \texttt{AND},
\texttt{OR}, \texttt{XOR} primitives so the harness can count gates
by monkey-patching).

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What the journey shows about FP4 circuit minimization}

Three observations stand out.

First, manual structural decomposition got us to within $\sim$25\%
of the eventual minimum but no further. The log-domain factorization
(Equation~\ref{eq:factorization}) is the right algebraic insight
and is unavoidable: every smaller circuit we found includes the
same E-sum / K-flag / decoder pipeline in some recognizable form.
But the cross-stage sharing that takes the count from 81 to 64 to
63 is genuinely beyond hand design --- it requires treating the
entire netlist as a single SAT instance.

Second, a single fortunate eSLIM window did the bulk of the work.
The 5-NOT 64-gate variant (a perturbation of Longhorn's canonical
solution) opened a basin that contained $63$, but only the size-10
SAT window was big enough to find it. Smaller windows iterated
indefinitely cannot escape the surrounding $64$--$66$ basin
(Figure~\ref{fig:chains}).

Third, the negative-result campaign matters. Without the
$\sim$280-run perturbation sweep, the alternative-$\sigma$ pyramid,
the ABC re-synthesis check, the iterative-chain divergence plot,
the ANF $691$-gate dead end, and the $24$-h CaDiCaL run on a
$250$\,M-clause $K{=}62$ encoding (Section~\ref{sec:k62}), we would
not be able to argue that $63$ represents anything more than a
lucky instance. With them, the case for empirical saturation at
$63$ is tight.

\subsection{What we did \emph{not} do}

We did not run wave-2 perturbation experiments
(Exp E on alternate locations, Exp A round-4 on the 4-NOT seed,
Exp F on 4-NOT canonical) because the saturation evidence makes
the prior-probability of cracking sub-63 by more of the same
strategy very low.
We did not run an LLM-guided rewriting experiment (originally
Track \#3) because that path requires an
\texttt{ANTHROPIC\_API\_KEY} we did not have configured.
We did run direct SAT exact synthesis at $K{=}62$ on a $64$\,GiB
GCP host (Section~\ref{sec:k62}), but did not run a Knuth/Kullmann
auxiliary-variable encoding (which would shrink the clause set by
roughly $10\times$) or a Kissat / cube-and-conquer parallel attack;
either is the natural next step on a larger compute budget.

\subsection{Open questions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Is $K = 62$ reachable in $\mathcal{G}$?}
        Open. The CaDiCaL run in Section~\ref{sec:k62} did not
        settle within $24$\,h on a $250$\,M-clause encoding. A
        decisive answer needs either a more compact encoding or a
        stronger / parallel solver. The empirical evidence (zero
        sub-63 across $\sim$$280$ heuristic runs, $11$ $\sigma$
        remaps, ABC re-synthesis, and iterative chains) leans
        moderately toward UNSAT, but this is conjecture, not proof.
  \item \textbf{Can a structural seed beat eSLIM windowed
        synthesis?}
        Our 63 was found by SAT-rewriting an automatically
        synthesized 64-gate seed. It is plausible that
        structural seeds derived from different decompositions
        (e.g.\ Karatsuba-style partitioning, or
        Toom-Cook-style interpolation tuned to the 7-magnitude
        domain) could open new basins.
  \item \textbf{Are there gate libraries
        $\mathcal{G}'$ supporting $K \le 60$?}
        Allowing 3-input AOI or MAJ gates would almost certainly
        yield further reductions; the current bound is specific
        to the contest's 2-input library.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

We verified a 63-gate FP4$\times$FP4$\to$QI9 multiplier in
$\mathcal{G} = \{\text{AND2}, \text{OR2}, \text{XOR2},
\text{NOT1}\}$, beating LonghornSilicon's previously published
64-gate solution by one gate.
The result was reached by combining manual structural decomposition
($288\to82$) with SAT-based local search using
eSLIM~\cite{reichl2024eslim} on a Longhorn-derived 64-gate netlist
($82\to81\to64\to63$). The key automated step was a single
\texttt{--size 10} SAT window applied to a five-NOT 64-gate variant
produced by XOR re-association.
A multi-pronged saturation campaign --- $280$+ perturbation runs,
$11$ alternative $\sigma$ remaps, ABC \texttt{deepsyn} re-synthesis,
iterative chains, ANF re-derivation, lower-bound analysis, and a
$24$-h direct PySAT/CaDiCaL exact-synthesis run at $K{=}62$ on a
$64$\,GiB GCP host (a $21{,}524$-variable, $250$\,M-clause encoding
that did not settle within budget) --- did not produce a sub-63
result. The empirical case for $63$ as the optimum in $\mathcal{G}$
is tight; a formal proof remains open and likely requires a more
compact encoding or a stronger / parallel solver.

All code, BLIFs, ledger TSVs, and decision logs are at

The 63-gate body itself is in \texttt{multiplier\_63.py};
